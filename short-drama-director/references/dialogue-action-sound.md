# Dialogue, Emotion, Action, and Sound

## Dialogue coverage

Keep source wording and punctuation. Estimate delivery before assigning time:

- 35-49 Chinese characters: inspect whether at least two visual covers are needed;
- 50-70: inspect whether at least three covers are needed;
- above 70: normally distribute across real shots or segments;
- an on-camera delivery above roughly 20 characters and 6 seconds needs visible performance change or alternate coverage.

Do not cut at every comma. Use scale change, a source-present prop/listener, blocking, or off-screen continuation. Never invent a listener or object.

## Emotion physicalization

Translate psychology into `trigger -> physiology/gaze -> body or prop action -> voice/pause -> next decision`. Use only source-supported intensity and keep quoted dialogue exact.

## Action design

For fights, pursuit, collision, and conflict, preserve `space -> preparation -> execution/contact -> result -> consequence -> re-anchor`. Keep aggressor, receiver, contact/result, exit, obstacle, and threat direction readable. Do not add unprovided injury or destruction.

## Sound

Use sound when it changes attention, motivates a cut, preserves information, or confirms action. Describe near/far, short/continuous, pitch range, material, rhythm, and echo objectively. Avoid subjective metaphors, exact decibels/frequencies, and unrequested music.

## Flashbacks and time jumps

Enter through a source-supported physical trigger, use only supplied memory content, and return through a physical anchor. Show time passage through source-supported changes in light, occupancy, object state, position, or fixed-composition jump cuts.

## AI executability

Reduce or split when a short shot combines three or more of: fast multi-person blocking, prop transfers, emotional reversal, large camera movement, long synced dialogue, or complex visual effect.
