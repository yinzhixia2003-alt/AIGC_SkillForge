# Staging, POV, and Special Shots

Use this reference for multi-person scenes, layered blocking, internal focus transfer, subjective shots, functional scene anchors, and unusual camera language.

## Multi-person and multi-action staging

Do not split by counting people or verbs. Keep a complex shot when it has one primary viewing task, the people/actions share one causal result, spatial relations remain legible, and the end state is verifiable.

Organize complexity with:

- foreground, middle ground, and background layers;
- distance, occlusion, and established entrances/exits;
- ordered blocking such as enter -> stop -> turn -> yield;
- sound order and dialogue handoff;
- low-amplitude secondary/background motion;
- focus transfer or a small motivated camera move.

Simplify or split when two core actions must be fully read at the same time, visual and sound centers compete, force/target/eyeline becomes ambiguous, a prop transfer loses owner/hand/end position, or long dialogue competes with blocking plus an emotional turn.

## Internal focus transfer

Use internal focus transfer only when:

1. The first viewing task finishes or visibly reduces.
2. The next subject is already spatially established.
3. A causal trigger hands attention over: pause, sound, gaze, blocking, reaction, or focus change.
4. The second task follows from the first and does not compete with it.

If both tasks need sustained lip sync, complex action, or separate space explanation, use a shot group instead.

## POV decision system

Use POV to limit information, reinforce a character's experience, or reveal the character's observation target. Prefer over-shoulder or semi-subjective coverage when the audience must retain both observer and target.

Default chain:

`observer reaction/eyeline -> POV or over-shoulder target -> observer reaction/action result`

Lock observer position, height, facing, eyeline, target direction/distance, active occlusion, and knowledge boundary. Do not place the camera where the observer cannot physically be, reveal unseen information, or make the observer's full frontal body appear in a pure POV. Hands/body edges may appear only when consistent with the approved action.

Emotional POV may use slight breath movement, brief defocus, or edge change only when source-supported. Avoid random shake and game-like first-person movement.

## Production anchors

- State anchor: show source-supported fatigue, injury, dirt, wetness, damage, or constrained movement; never beautify it away or exaggerate it.
- Prop/UI anchor: lock quantity, owner, hand, position, and state. Keep unspecified screens/UI unreadable.
- Functional scene anchor: establish the route, door/window/vehicle, obstacles, usable movement area, and fixed objects needed by the action. Avoid decorative detail with no narrative function.

## Special shots

Mirrors, reflections, dives, orbits, spins, whip pans, rapid focus shifts, and aggressive subjective movement require all of:

1. A clear narrative value.
2. Known identity, viewing direction, and spatial position.
3. Preserved axis, action result, and primary viewing task.
4. Reduced competing dialogue/action when the camera behavior is complex.

Do not use a special shot merely to enrich camera language. If it cannot satisfy these conditions, use a simpler angle or fixed camera without changing the story beat.
